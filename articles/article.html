Neural Decoding Breakthrough Achieves 94% Accuracy in Speech Prediction from Cortical Signals

DR
Dr Rebecca Thornton
UCL BCI Lab 14 February 2026 5 min read

Researchers at the UCL Brain-Computer Interface Lab, in collaboration with the University of Oxford and the Alan Turing Institute, have demonstrated a neural speech decoding system that achieves 94.3% accuracy on a 500-word vocabulary — surpassing the previous state of the art by more than 11 percentage points while operating in real time on a standard clinical-grade EEG headset.

Key Finding

94.3% phoneme classification accuracy from a 64-channel EEG cap, enabling a decoded typing speed of approximately 62 words per minute in healthy volunteers.

Background: The Challenge of Brain-to-Speech

For individuals living with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), locked-in syndrome, or severe dysarthria, the ability to communicate is profoundly compromised. Conventional augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices rely on residual motor control — eye tracking, switch scanning — which becomes inadequate as disease progresses.

Brain-computer interfaces that decode intended speech directly from neural signals offer a pathway to restore natural communication without requiring any motor output. However, the performance of non-invasive EEG-based speech BCIs had historically lagged behind invasive cortical implants by a wide margin, limiting their clinical translation.

Figure 1 — The signal processing pipeline from 64-channel EEG capture through transformer-based decoding to text output. Credit: Thornton et al., Nature Neuroscience, 2026.

Methodology

Dataset and Participants

Thirty-two healthy volunteers participated in up to 10 recording sessions each, producing a cumulative 480 hours of imagined speech EEG data. Participants silently attempted to articulate words from a pre-specified vocabulary while their scalp EEG was recorded at 2048 Hz using a clinical EEG cap.

Crucially, the study also enrolled six participants with ALS at varying disease stages, representing the first prospective ALS cohort in a non-invasive speech BCI study of this scale within the UK.

The Temporal Transformer Architecture

The decoding model, dubbed CortexLM, is a causal transformer architecture adapted from large language model design principles. It ingests 500 ms windows of pre-processed EEG and outputs a probability distribution over phonemes, which a beam-search decoder then converts to word hypotheses constrained by a language model prior.

"We essentially gave the network the same architectural primitives that power modern LLMs, but trained it on the temporal structure of cortical oscillations rather than text tokens. The gains speak for themselves."

— Dr Rebecca Thornton, lead author, UCL BCI Lab

Results

Across the 32 healthy participants, CortexLM achieved a mean phoneme accuracy of 94.3% ± 2.1% and a word error rate (WER) of 6.8% on the held-out test vocabulary. Decoded typing speed averaged 62 words per minute (WPM), with the fastest participant reaching 81 WPM — competitive with eye-tracking interfaces in optimal conditions.

Figure 2 — Comparison of word error rate across published non-invasive BCI speech decoders (2018–2026). CortexLM represents the 2026 result. Credit: Thornton et al., 2026.

In the ALS cohort, performance was naturally lower (mean WER 18.4%) but remained practically useful for functional communication, with participants self-reporting high satisfaction scores on the Communication Effectiveness Index.

Implications and Next Steps

The team plans to commence a multi-site clinical feasibility trial across three NHS hospitals in Q3 2026, supported by an MRC Developmental Pathway Funding Scheme award. Regulatory engagement with the MHRA has already begun.

  • Expansion to a 1,000-word vocabulary target by end of 2026
  • Integration with off-the-shelf EEG hardware to reduce system cost below £3,000
  • Open-sourcing the CortexLM weights and pre-processing pipeline on GitHub
  • Longitudinal follow-up of ALS participants over 18 months

The full paper, "High-accuracy imagined speech decoding from scalp EEG via causal transformer networks", is published in Nature Neuroscience (DOI: 10.1038/s41593-026-00000-0) and is available open-access.
